# Lean Startup: Validate Before You Scale

Distilled from Eric Ries, The Lean Startup. The method treats a new venture as a
series of experiments, not a plan to execute. You are not testing whether you can
build the thing. You are testing whether anyone wants it.

## When to use

Use when you face deep uncertainty about whether a product, feature, or business
idea will work, and you can run a cheap test to find out. Decision cue: if you
can name the riskiest assumption that would kill the idea, and you have not yet
checked it against a real customer, you are in Lean Startup territory.

## Decision tree

- If you have not built anything yet and do not know if anyone wants it, then
  run Build-Measure-Learn around your single riskiest assumption. Start here.
- If customers use the product but you cannot tell if it is getting better, then
  fix your metrics first (see Scored checklist, actionable metrics). Vanity
  numbers hide the truth.
- If three or more experiments show flat or negative results on your core metric,
  then consider a pivot. Do not keep tuning a dead engine.
- If experiments show steady improvement on a real cohort metric, then persevere
  and pour fuel on the validated engine of growth.
- If the idea is already validated and you need to scale operations or raise
  money, then this skill is the wrong tool. Move to a growth or fundraising play.
- If your uncertainty is about market structure or competitive moats rather than
  customer demand, then a positioning or strategy framework fits better.

## Core framework

The Build-Measure-Learn loop. The goal is to minimize total time through the
loop, not to maximize output of any single step.

1. State the leap-of-faith assumption. Name the one belief that, if false, sinks
   the idea. Usually it is the value hypothesis (do customers get value) or the
   growth hypothesis (will adoption spread).
2. Define the experiment. Decide what result would confirm or refute the
   assumption, and pick the actionable metric you will read. Write the pass and
   fail thresholds before you build.
3. Build the minimum viable product. The MVP is the smallest thing that produces
   a real signal from real customers. It can be a landing page, a concierge run
   you do by hand, or a single feature. Build only what the experiment needs.
4. Measure with cohort-based, actionable metrics. Track behavior of a defined
   group over time. Avoid vanity totals (cumulative signups, raw page views)
   that always go up and prove nothing.
5. Learn, then decide: persevere or pivot. If the metric moves, keep the engine
   and tune it. If it does not after honest effort, pivot: change one structural
   element (the customer, the problem, the channel, the business model) while
   keeping what you learned.
6. Shorten the loop and repeat. Each pass should be cheaper and faster than the
   last. Speed of learning is the competitive edge.

## Scored checklist

Score your current experiment. Each criterion is worth 1 point.

1. Riskiest assumption named (1 pt): you can state in one sentence the belief
   that would kill the idea if false.
2. Falsifiable thresholds set before building (1 pt): you wrote the pass and
   fail numbers in advance, not after seeing results.
3. MVP is genuinely minimal (1 pt): you can point to features you cut because
   the experiment did not need them.
4. Real customers, not your team (1 pt): the signal comes from people who could
   actually buy, not from internal opinion.
5. Actionable metric, not vanity (1 pt): the metric ties to a cause you control
   and reads a cohort over time.
6. Loop completed in days or weeks, not quarters (1 pt): the test finished fast
   enough to inform the next move.
7. Pivot-or-persevere decision recorded (1 pt): you made and logged an explicit
   call, not a quiet drift.

Total: 7. Pass threshold: 5 or higher. Below 5, your experiment will produce
ambiguous data. Tighten the weakest criterion before you build.

## Honest limitations

- Weak for deep-tech and long-horizon bets. An MVP cannot validate a drug, a
  chip, or a fusion reactor. Some value only appears at full scale or after years
  of R&D, and a thin slice misleads more than it informs.
- The MVP can damage brand and trust. A deliberately rough release in a market
  that expects polish (medical, financial, luxury) can lose customers you never
  win back. The method underweights reputation cost.
- Metric obsession crowds out vision. Teams optimize the measurable and abandon
  the bold idea that no single experiment can prove. Local optimization is not
  the same as a category-defining product.
- Pivot can become an excuse to quit early. Three flat experiments may mean the
  idea is wrong, or may mean you ran three bad experiments. The framework gives
  little help telling those apart.
- Commonly misapplied as "ship junk fast." Minimum viable is about learning, not
  about low quality. An MVP with no measurement plan is just an unfinished
  product.

## Conflicts with other frameworks

- Blue Ocean / category creation. Lean says test demand with a small MVP and
  pivot off weak signal. A genuine new category often shows weak early signal
  because customers cannot yet articulate the need. Resolve by situation: in a
  known market with comparable products, trust the Lean signal. When you are
  creating a category, weight qualitative discovery and founder conviction higher,
  and use the MVP to shape the offer rather than to kill it.
- Crossing the Chasm. Chasm theory says win one beachhead segment with a whole,
  complete product; Lean says ship the minimum. Resolve by stage: use Lean MVPs
  before product-market fit to find the segment, then switch to the chasm play
  and ship a complete product once you target the beachhead.

## Example

A two-person team believes small law firms will pay for AI that drafts routine
contracts.

1. Riskiest assumption: lawyers will trust and pay for AI-drafted contracts. The
   value hypothesis, not the build.
2. Experiment: offer the service to 20 firms. Pass if 5 or more pay a deposit
   for a paid pilot. Fail if fewer than 2 do.
3. MVP: no product. A one-page site plus a human drafting each contract by hand
   behind the scenes (a concierge MVP). They cut the entire automated pipeline
   they assumed they needed.
4. Measure: of 20 firms contacted, 6 pay a deposit. The cohort metric is
   deposit-paying firms per 20 contacted, not website visits.
5. Learn: value hypothesis holds. Persevere. The next loop tests whether they can
   automate the drafting and keep quality, the build risk they deferred.
6. Shorter next loop: ship one automated contract type to the 6 paying firms and
   measure edit rate before and after.

Checklist score for this experiment: 7 of 7. The team spent two weeks and zero
engineering to learn what a six-month build would have taught them too late.
