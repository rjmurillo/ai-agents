# Four Steps to the Epiphany

A startup is not a small version of a big company. It is a search for a business
model that works. This skill applies Steve Blank's Customer Development method to
that search. The goal of the early steps is learning, not revenue. You leave the
building, test your guesses against real buyers, and refuse to scale until the
evidence says the model holds.

## When to use

Use this when you have a product idea or early build and need to find the first
customers who will actually pay, not just nod politely. Decision cue: if you are
about to hire a sales team, run paid ads, or pour money into growth before any
stranger has paid you, stop and run this first.

## Decision tree

- If you have no validated problem yet (only your own conviction): run Customer
  Discovery. Do not build features.
- If buyers confirm the problem but you have not closed a sale to a stranger: run
  Customer Validation. Sell by hand before you automate selling.
- If you have a repeatable, scalable sales motion proven on real strangers: move
  to Customer Creation (demand generation) and Company Building. This skill ends here.
- If your market is genuinely new and no buyer recognizes the problem yet: stay
  in Discovery longer; you are educating, not validating.
- If you keep "pivoting" weekly with no new evidence: you are guessing, not
  testing. Write falsifiable hypotheses first, then go test them.

## Core framework

The method has four steps. This skill covers the first two in depth; the last two
are the reward for passing the first two.

1. **State your hypotheses.** Write down, in one page, your guesses: who the
   customer is, what problem they have, what they pay today, and why your solution
   wins. These are guesses, not facts. Label them so.
2. **Customer Discovery: test the problem.** Leave the building. Interview 15 to
   30 target buyers. Do not pitch. Ask how they work today, what the problem costs
   them, and what they have tried. You are testing whether the pain is real and
   urgent, not whether they like your idea.
3. **Customer Discovery: test the solution.** Only after the problem holds, show a
   rough solution (mockup, demo, concierge). Watch for buying signals: they ask
   the price, want a timeline, offer to pilot. Polite interest is a fail.
4. **Customer Validation: sell by hand.** The founder sells, in person, to
   strangers (not friends or advisors). Each close teaches the sales script,
   objections, and the real buyer. A signed order or paid pilot from a stranger is
   the milestone.
5. **Pivot or proceed.** If the evidence contradicts a hypothesis, pivot that one
   guess and re-test. Do not abandon the whole plan over one bad interview. Proceed
   only when problem and solution both pass and a stranger has paid.
6. **Then scale (out of scope here).** Customer Creation builds demand; Company
   Building replaces founder-led search with a functional organization.

## Scored checklist

Score each item. 2 points unless noted. Pass threshold: 9 of 12.

- [ ] You wrote falsifiable hypotheses (customer, problem, solution) before any
  interviews. (1 pt)
- [ ] You interviewed at least 15 target buyers face to face or by call, not by
  survey. (2 pts)
- [ ] In interviews you spent more time listening than pitching (problem first,
  solution last). (2 pts)
- [ ] At least 5 buyers described the problem as urgent and named what it costs
  them. (2 pts)
- [ ] You showed a solution and saw a concrete buying signal (price question,
  pilot offer, timeline ask) from a stranger. (2 pts)
- [ ] A stranger (not a friend, investor, or advisor) committed money or a paid
  pilot. (2 pts)
- [ ] You logged at least one hypothesis that failed and how you changed it. (1 pt)

Total: 12. Pass: 9 or higher. Below 9, you are still guessing; stay in Discovery.

## Honest limitations

- **Built for the 2000s enterprise-sales world.** The original assumes a defined
  market and identifiable buyers. For brand-new markets, social products, or
  consumer apps where users cannot articulate the problem, interviews mislead.
  Lean Startup's MVP-and-metrics loop often fits better there.
- **Interviews are weak evidence.** People say one thing and do another. A "yes,
  I would buy that" is not a sale. Weight behavior (paid pilots, signed orders)
  far above words.
- **Founder-led selling does not always generalize.** A charismatic founder can
  close deals a normal rep never could. Validation can look real and still fail to
  scale. Test whether a non-founder can close before declaring victory.
- **Sample bias.** Thirty interviews you sourced from your own network are not a
  market. Mix in cold strangers or the signal is your bubble talking back.

## Conflicts with other frameworks

- **Lean Startup (build-measure-learn).** Lean says ship an MVP and read metrics;
  Four Steps says interview before you build. Resolve by market type: for known
  buyers and high-cost mistakes (enterprise, hardware, regulated), interview first
  per Four Steps. For cheap-to-build consumer or web products where behavior is
  the only honest signal, ship the MVP and measure.
- **Buy-vs-build / TCO frameworks.** Those optimize a sourcing decision once the
  problem is known. Four Steps runs earlier: do not run a build-vs-buy analysis on
  a problem no stranger has confirmed. Validate the problem first, then decide how
  to source the solution.

## Example

A founder builds a tool to auto-generate compliance reports for hospital IT teams.

- **Hypotheses:** buyer is the hospital IT compliance lead; problem is 20 hours a
  month of manual report assembly; today they use spreadsheets; the tool saves the
  20 hours.
- **Discovery (problem):** 18 interviews. 12 confirm the manual pain. But 9 say
  the real blocker is auditor sign-off, not assembly time. Hypothesis fails on the
  problem. Pivot: the urgent problem is audit-readiness, not assembly speed.
- **Discovery (solution):** re-show the tool reframed around audit-ready output.
  4 leads ask the price; 2 ask for a pilot. Buying signal present.
- **Validation:** founder sells to a stranger hospital (not a contact). They pay
  for a 60-day pilot. Milestone hit.
- **Score:** hypotheses written (1), 18 interviews (2), listened first (2), 5+
  urgent (2), buying signal (2), stranger paid (2), logged the audit pivot (1).
  Total 12 of 12. Proceed to Customer Creation.

The win was not the first hypothesis. It was catching that the stated problem
(assembly time) was the wrong one before spending a year building for it.
