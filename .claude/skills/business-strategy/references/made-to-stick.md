# Made to Stick

Distilled from Chip and Dan Heath, "Made to Stick." Original synthesis. The
book's claim: ideas survive not because they are correct but because they are
built to be remembered. Memorability is an engineering problem, not a talent.

## When to use

Use when you have a message that is accurate but does not stick: people nod,
then forget it by next week. Decision cue: if you cannot say your idea in one
sentence and have a stranger repeat it back tomorrow, this skill applies.

Do not use to decide what is true. This makes a message memorable, not correct.
Validate the claim first, then make it stick.

## Decision tree

- If the audience does not yet understand the idea at all, then start with
  Simple (find the core) before anything else.
- If they understand but do not believe it, then lead with Credible (a vivid
  detail, a testable claim, a source they trust).
- If they believe it but do not feel it, then lead with Emotional (one named
  person they can care about, not a statistic).
- If they feel it but do not remember it, then lead with Unexpected (break a
  pattern, open a curiosity gap).
- If they remember it but cannot retell it, then lead with Concrete and Story
  (sensory language plus a narrative they can repeat).
- If you are writing for experts, watch for the Curse of Knowledge: you know too
  much to feel what they are missing. Run the Scored checklist below.

## Core framework

The SUCCESs framework. Six attributes of a sticky idea. You rarely need all six;
pick the two or three that fix your audience's actual gap.

1. **Find the core (Simple).** Strip the idea to its single most important
   truth. Not dumbed down: prioritized. Ask "if they remember one thing, what is
   it?" Cut every other point that competes with that one. A proverb is the
   target shape: short, deep, repeatable.
2. **Break the pattern (Unexpected).** Surprise gets attention; a curiosity gap
   keeps it. Find the counterintuitive angle. Name a gap in what the audience
   knows, then close it. Avoid gimmick surprise that does not serve the core.
3. **Make it tangible (Concrete).** Replace abstractions with things people can
   picture, touch, or do. Concrete language lets different people build the same
   mental image. Numbers become objects; concepts become examples.
4. **Earn belief (Credible).** Give the idea a reason to be trusted. Use vivid
   specific detail, a source the audience respects, or a "try it yourself" test
   the audience can run themselves. Internal credibility beats borrowed
   authority.
5. **Make them care (Emotional).** People feel for one identifiable person, not
   for a mass or a statistic. Tie the idea to identity ("people like us do
   this") or self-interest stated concretely. Drop the abstraction; name a face.
6. **Tell a story (Stories).** A story is a flight simulator: it lets the
   audience rehearse the idea. Use the Challenge plot (underdog), Connection plot
   (bridge a gap), or Creativity plot (solve a puzzle). The story carries the
   other five attributes.

The hidden seventh element is the enemy: the **Curse of Knowledge.** Once you
know something, you cannot remember not knowing it, so you skip the concrete
detail the audience needs. Every step above is a counter to that curse.

## Scored checklist

Score the message you are about to send. Each yes is worth 1 point.

- [ ] **Core (2 pts):** Can you state the single most important idea in one
  sentence, and have you cut the competing points?
- [ ] **Repeatable (1 pt):** Could a listener repeat the core to a third party a
  day later without notes?
- [ ] **Unexpected (1 pt):** Does it break an assumption or open a curiosity gap
  in the first 15 seconds?
- [ ] **Concrete (2 pts):** Is there at least one image, object, or example a
  reader can picture, with no jargon standing in for it?
- [ ] **Credible (1 pt):** Is there a specific detail, trusted source, or
  self-test that earns belief?
- [ ] **Emotional (1 pt):** Does it name one person or identity to care about
  instead of a statistic?
- [ ] **Story (1 pt):** Is there a narrative the audience can mentally rehearse?
- [ ] **Curse check (1 pt):** Did you test it on someone outside your expertise
  and confirm they got the core?

Total: 10 points. **Pass threshold: 7.** Below 7, the message is accurate but
forgettable; fix the lowest-scoring attribute first.

## Honest limitations

- **Stickiness is not truth.** The same techniques make false ideas, urban
  legends, and propaganda memorable. The book is a manual for transmission, not
  for accuracy. A sticky wrong idea is worse than a forgettable right one.
- **Not all six, always.** Forcing every attribute bloats the message and buries
  the core. Two or three well-chosen attributes beat six crammed in.
- **Weak on persuading the resistant.** Made to Stick optimizes memory and
  comprehension, not changing a committed opponent's mind. For active
  resistance, you need negotiation or framing tools, not just stickiness.
- **Anecdote-heavy evidence.** The book's case studies are selected after the
  fact (survivorship bias). Use the framework as a generative checklist, not as
  proof that any single tactic will work.
- **Common misapplication:** treating "concrete" as "add a chart." Charts are
  often abstract. Concrete means a thing a person can picture, not more data.

## Conflicts with other frameworks

- **vs. a data-and-rigor framework (decision-critic, pre-mortem):** Those
  demand statistics, base rates, and disconfirming evidence. Made to Stick says
  one named person beats a statistic. Resolve by audience: use rigor to decide
  what is true (internal, analytical audience), then use stickiness to transmit
  the conclusion (external, time-poor audience). Never let stickiness override
  the rigor step; transmit only what survived it.
- **vs. a negotiation or framing framework:** Negotiation optimizes for leverage
  and concession paths against a counterparty with opposed interests. Made to
  Stick assumes a receptive or neutral audience you are informing. Resolve by
  stance: if the other side has something to lose by agreeing, lead with
  negotiation and use stickiness only to make your one anchor memorable.

## Example

Weak version (engineer to executives): "Our caching layer reduces P99 latency
by introducing a read-through invalidation strategy that improves throughput
under load."

Apply SUCCESs:

- **Core:** "Pages load before users notice."
- **Concrete + Credible:** "Today the dashboard takes 4.2 seconds. After the
  change it takes 0.6. Open it on your phone after we ship and time it."
- **Emotional:** "Maria, the ops lead, opens this 40 times a shift. We are
  giving her back 2 minutes every shift."
- **Unexpected:** "We are not buying faster servers. We are deleting work we
  were doing twice."
- **Story:** "Last quarter a customer bounced because the page hung. Same page,
  same code path. This fixes that path."

Sticky version: "We are deleting work we did twice, so the dashboard goes from
4.2 seconds to 0.6. Open it on your phone after we ship and time it. Maria gets
2 minutes back every shift." Score: 8/10. It passes.
