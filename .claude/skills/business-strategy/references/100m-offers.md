# 100M Offers

Distillation of Alex Hormozi's "$100M Offers". The method: stop competing on
price by making the offer itself the differentiator. A commodity sold the same
way as rivals forces a price war. A differentiated offer escapes the comparison
entirely.

## When to use

Use this when you have a product or service and need to construct the thing you
actually sell: the bundle, the price, the promise, and the reasons to buy now.

Decision cue: if a prospect can put your offer side by side with a competitor's
and pick on price alone, you have a commodity, not an offer. Build the offer first.

## Decision tree

- If you have no paying customers yet and no proof, then nail the market and the
  core promise before stacking value. Skip enhancement until the base offer
  converts a few buyers.
- If buyers say "too expensive," then you have a value problem, not a price
  problem. Raise perceived value with the Value Equation, do not cut price.
- If buyers stall or "think about it," then add urgency and scarcity and a
  guarantee that removes the risk of saying yes.
- If you compete in a crowded market on identical features, then differentiate
  by naming a specific dream outcome and a unique mechanism, not a discount.
- If your margins are thin and you discount to win, then re-bundle and reprice
  upward; a higher price with more value beats a lower price with less.
- If demand already exceeds supply at current price, then raise price first; that
  is the fastest profit lever you have.

## Core framework

The Grand Slam Offer. An offer so differentiated that prospects buy regardless
of price and you escape commodity comparison.

1. Pick a starving market. Demand beats everything. Choose a market with pain,
   purchasing power, easy to target, and growing. A great offer to the wrong
   market still fails.
2. Charge a premium price. High price forces high perceived value, funds
   delivery and acquisition, and filters out low-commitment buyers. Price is a
   marketing decision, not a cost-plus calculation.
3. Maximize the Value Equation. Perceived value rises when you increase the
   dream outcome and the likelihood of achieving it, and decrease the time to
   results and the effort and sacrifice required. Move all four levers; the two
   denominators (time, effort) usually move value the most.
4. Build the offer by solving every obstacle. List every reason the dream
   outcome would fail for the buyer. Turn each obstacle into a deliverable that
   removes it. The offer is the sum of solved problems, not a feature list.
5. Trim and stack. Cut low-value, high-cost deliverables. Keep high-value,
   low-cost ones. Present what remains as a stack with each piece priced, so the
   total named value dwarfs the asking price.
6. Enhance with the four amplifiers. Add scarcity (limited supply), urgency
   (deadline), bonuses (each solving a specific objection), and a guarantee that
   reverses risk. These convert a good offer into one that is hard to refuse.
7. Name it. Give the offer a clear, benefit-led name tied to the avatar and the
   outcome, so it is memorable and easy to say yes to.

## Scored checklist

Score the offer before you launch it. Each criterion is worth 2 points.

- [ ] Market is a clear, reachable, paying segment with real pain (2)
- [ ] Price is premium and defended by value, not set by cost-plus (2)
- [ ] Dream outcome is specific and stated in the buyer's words (2)
- [ ] Time to result and effort required are visibly reduced (2)
- [ ] Every major obstacle maps to a named deliverable that removes it (2)
- [ ] At least one risk-reversal guarantee is present and concrete (2)
- [ ] Scarcity or urgency is real, not fabricated (2)
- [ ] Named total value of the stack is several times the asking price (2)

Total: 16. Pass threshold: 12. Below 12, the offer still looks like a commodity;
return to the weakest criterion before spending on traffic.

## Honest limitations

- Survivorship and single source. The method comes from one operator's wins in
  gyms, agencies, and info-products. It generalizes well to high-margin services
  and digital products, less well to low-margin retail, regulated B2B with
  procurement cycles, or enterprise sales where price is committee-controlled.
- Fake scarcity erodes trust. Manufactured countdown timers and "only 3 left"
  lies train buyers to distrust you and can breach consumer-protection law.
  Scarcity must be true.
- Guarantees attract abuse. Strong risk reversal raises conversion but invites
  refunds and bad-fit buyers. Pair it with qualification, or margin evaporates.
- Premium pricing assumes you can deliver. Charging more without raising actual
  outcomes is a short con that kills referrals and reputation.
- Value is perceived, not asserted. Stacking named dollar amounts that buyers do
  not believe reads as a gimmick and lowers trust rather than raising value.

## Conflicts with other frameworks

- Versus a pricing or unit-economics framework. 100M Offers pushes you to raise
  price for perceived value; a margin or LTV/CAC framework may say the higher
  price suppresses volume below breakeven. Resolve by situation: in a
  demand-constrained or trust-light market, validate willingness to pay with a
  small cohort before repricing the whole funnel; let unit economics veto a
  price that starves acquisition.
- Versus a positioning or category-design framework. This skill differentiates
  through the offer bundle; a positioning framework differentiates through
  category and narrative. They collide when a clever bundle papers over a weak
  category fit. Resolve by sequence: fix category and target customer first
  (positioning), then construct the Grand Slam Offer inside that position. An
  irresistible offer aimed at the wrong category still misses.

## Example

A freelance React developer sells "$95/hour, custom web apps" and loses every
bid to cheaper offshore shops.

Rebuild as a Grand Slam Offer:

- Market: funded seed-stage startups that need an MVP in weeks, have cash, and
  feel the pain of a slipping launch.
- Dream outcome: "A working, investor-demo-ready MVP in 30 days, or it is free."
- Value Equation moves: cut time (30-day fixed window), cut effort (handles
  hosting, CI, and design system so the founder does nothing technical), raise
  likelihood (shows three prior MVPs that raised follow-on rounds).
- Obstacles solved into deliverables: "will it be late" answered by the
  guarantee; "will I get locked in" answered by a handover doc and full source;
  "will it scale" answered by a load-tested baseline.
- Stack named value: build (8k), design system (3k), CI and deploy (2k),
  30-day support (2k). Named value 15k, price 12k.
- Amplifiers: takes two MVP clients per month (real capacity scarcity); 30-day
  delivery deadline (urgency); free-if-late guarantee (risk reversal).
- Name: "The 30-Day Investor-Ready MVP."

Checklist score: market 2, premium price 2, specific outcome 2, time and effort
reduced 2, obstacles mapped 2, guarantee 2, real scarcity 2, value over price 2.
Total 16, passes. The buyer is no longer comparing hourly rates; they are buying
a dated, guaranteed outcome.
