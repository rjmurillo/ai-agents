# The Mom Test

Customer conversations lie to you by default. People want to be nice, so they
praise your idea, promise to buy, and predict their own future behavior. None of
that is data. This skill turns a conversation into evidence by keeping you off
the topic of your idea and on the topic of the person's real life.

## When to use

Use this when you are about to talk to a potential customer, user, or buyer and
you need to learn whether a problem is real before you build a solution for it.
Decision cue: if you catch yourself about to describe your idea and watch for a
reaction, stop and run this instead.

## Decision tree

- If you have not yet talked to anyone in the target group, then start here in
  Discovery mode: dig into their workflow and past behavior, no pitch.
- If you already have an idea or prototype, then stay in Validation mode but do
  not demo first. Probe the problem, then let commitment reveal itself.
- If you only want a yes/no on "is this a good idea," then this is the wrong
  tool. The idea is yours to judge; the facts of their life are what you gather.
- If you need a pricing or willingness-to-pay signal, then ask for a real
  commitment (time, money, reputation), not a hypothetical "would you pay X."
- If the person keeps complimenting you, then you have lost the thread. Redirect
  to specifics: "When did you last hit this? Walk me through it."
- If you are recruiting interviewees or scaling outreach, then pair this with a
  separate go-to-market or sales motion; this skill covers the conversation,
  not the funnel.

## Core framework

1. Talk about their life, not your idea. Open on their work, their goals, their
   last bad day in this area. Never lead with what you are building.
2. Ask about specifics in the past, not generics about the future. "How do you
   handle X today?" beats "Would you use a tool that did X?" Memory is data;
   prediction is fiction.
3. Dig for the last time it happened. Concrete episodes expose real cost: time
   lost, money spent, workarounds built. Vague "I always struggle with this" is
   not yet evidence.
4. Chase the emotion and the workaround. Strong feeling plus an existing hack
   (a spreadsheet, a hired person, a manual ritual) signals a problem worth
   money. No workaround usually means no real pain.
5. Deflect compliments; mine for commitment and advancement. Praise is the sound
   of a conversation going nowhere. Ask for something costly instead: an intro,
   a follow-up meeting, a pre-order, time to test.
6. Anchor every claim to a number or an action. "It is a nightmare" is a feeling;
   "I spend four hours every Friday on it" is a fact you can act on.
7. Take notes verbatim and review for facts versus fluff. Separate what they did
   (fact) from what they said they would do (fluff) before you draw conclusions.

## Scored checklist

Score each item after an interview. 1 point each unless noted.

- [ ] You spent most of the conversation on their life, not your idea. (1)
- [ ] You asked about a specific past event, not a hypothetical future. (1)
- [ ] You uncovered at least one concrete number: time, money, frequency. (2)
- [ ] You found an existing workaround they already use or pay for. (2)
- [ ] You deflected or ignored every compliment instead of banking it. (1)
- [ ] You asked for a real commitment and recorded the actual response. (2)
- [ ] You wrote down what they did, separate from what they said they would do. (1)

Total possible: 10. Pass threshold: 7 or higher. Below 7, the interview leaned on
opinion and prediction; treat its conclusions as unproven and run another.

## Honest limitations

- It tells you whether a problem is real. It does not tell you whether your
  specific solution wins, nor whether you can reach buyers cheaply. Problem
  validation is not market validation.
- It is weak on quantitative scale. A handful of deep interviews surfaces pain
  but cannot size a market. Pair it with surveys or usage data for that.
- It is easy to misapply as "never mention your idea." You do reveal the idea,
  just last, after you have the facts, and only to extract a commitment.
- Past behavior under-predicts genuinely new categories. If you are creating a
  behavior that did not exist, "what do you do today" returns thin signal; lean
  harder on the emotion and the cost of the current gap.
- Small samples invite confirmation bias. Talking to friends or to people who
  already like you produces flattery the method is designed to filter out, so
  bad sampling defeats it.

## Conflicts with other frameworks

- Against a Lean Startup build-measure-learn skill: that approach pushes you to
  ship an MVP fast and read the metrics. The Mom Test says talk before you build.
  Resolve by sequence: run interviews in Discovery to pick the right thing, then
  hand off to build-measure-learn once a problem clears the pass threshold.
- Against a value-proposition or jobs-to-be-done design skill: those frameworks
  encourage presenting a crafted value prop and testing resonance. That invites
  the exact compliments this skill distrusts. Resolve by situation: use the Mom
  Test to discover the job and its cost first, then use value-prop design to
  shape and test messaging once the job is confirmed.

## Example

You want to build a tool that helps freelance designers chase late invoices.

Wrong (pitch and predict): "I am building an app that auto-chases late client
invoices. Would you use that?" The designer, being kind, says "Yeah, that sounds
useful, I'd totally try it." You leave excited with zero evidence.

Right (Mom Test): "How do you handle invoicing right now? When was the last time
a client paid late?" She says, "Last month. A client went sixty days over. I sent
three reminder emails, then called twice. I keep a spreadsheet tab just to track
who owes me, and I lost about a week of cash flow I had counted on."

Now you have facts: a recent episode, a number (sixty days, a week of cash flow),
and a workaround (the spreadsheet, the manual emails and calls). You deflect her
later "this is a cool idea" and instead ask for commitment: "Can I send you the
spreadsheet replacement next week and have you run your real overdue invoices
through it?" She says yes and books the time. That booked time, not the
compliment, is your signal. Score this interview: specific past event (1),
numbers (2), workaround (2), commitment recorded (2), life over idea (1), notes
separated (1), compliment deflected (1) equals 10 of 10. The problem is real;
proceed to build.
