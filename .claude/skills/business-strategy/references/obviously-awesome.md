# Obviously Awesome: Positioning and Category Choice

Positioning is the context you set so a buyer understands what your product is, who
it is for, and why it beats the alternatives they already have in mind. Get it
right and the product looks obvious. Get it wrong and a good product reads as
confusing, overpriced, or pointless.

## When to use

Reach for this when you must pick or change the market category a product competes
in, or when a product is good but buyers do not understand it fast enough.

Decision cue: if a prospect's first reaction is "what is this?" or "why would I pay
for that?", the problem is usually position, not feature set. Fix position first.

## Decision tree

- If you are naming a brand-new product and have no buyers yet, skip to Core
  framework. You are positioning from a blank slate.
- If sales calls stall on "I do not get what this is", you have a category problem.
  Run the full framework, focus hardest on step 5 (market category).
- If buyers understand the product but pick a competitor, you have a differentiation
  problem. Focus on steps 2 and 3 (unique attributes and the value they create).
- If buyers love a demo but say "I have nothing to compare this to", you may need a
  new or sub category. See Core framework step 6 and Honest limitations.
- If the product sells fine and you just want a tagline, this is the wrong skill.
  Positioning is the strategy under the message, not the message itself.
- If you are deciding whether to build the capability at all, use buy-vs-build-framework
  first, then return here once the product exists.

## Core framework

Dunford's method works backward from competitive alternatives, then forward to the
category. Run the steps in order. Do not skip to category first.

1. List the competitive alternatives. Write down what a buyer would actually use if
   your product did not exist. Include "do nothing", spreadsheets, and a manual
   process, not just named competitors. These alternatives set the buyer's frame.
2. Isolate your unique attributes. Find the features and capabilities you have that
   the alternatives lack. Be specific and factual. An attribute is something you can
   demonstrate, not a claim about being better.
3. Map attributes to value. For each unique attribute, state the concrete benefit it
   delivers. Push past the feature to the outcome the buyer cares about.
4. Find who cares the most. Identify the buyer segment for whom that value is urgent
   and obvious. The best customers are the ones whose problem your value solves
   sharply, not the largest possible audience.
5. Choose the market category that frames the value. Pick the context that makes your
   value the obvious choice. The category sets buyer expectations: pricing, features
   they will look for, and who they compare you to. A wrong category buries your
   strengths next to competitors who beat you on the wrong axis.
6. Layer on a trend only if it helps. Connect to a relevant market trend to make the
   product feel current and timely. Skip this if the trend is hype the buyer distrusts.
   A forced trend reads as desperate.

Sequence matters: alternatives define the frame, attributes define your edge, value
and audience define who buys, and category is the deliberate choice that makes all of
it land. Categories are not given. You choose one.

## Scored checklist

Score each criterion. 1 point if yes, 0 if no. Total 7. Pass at 5 or more.

1. Have you listed real competitive alternatives, including "do nothing" and manual
   workarounds, not just named vendors? (1 pt)
2. Can you name at least three unique attributes you can demonstrate, not just claim? (1 pt)
3. Does each unique attribute map to a concrete buyer outcome, not a feature
   restatement? (1 pt)
4. Have you identified the specific segment that cares most, and can you describe them
   in one sentence? (1 pt)
5. Have you chosen a market category on purpose, and can you state why it frames your
   value better than the obvious default category? (1 pt)
6. Does the chosen category set buyer expectations you can meet on price and core
   features? (1 pt)
7. Can a target buyer restate, in their own words, what the product is and why it is for
   them after a 30 second pitch? (1 pt)

Below 5: positioning is not ready. The most common gap is criterion 5, a category
chosen by default rather than by deliberate choice.

## Honest limitations

- It assumes you already have a product and some buyer reactions to learn from. For a
  pure idea with zero feedback, the attribute and audience steps are guesses.
- Creating a brand-new category is expensive and slow. Dunford warns that category
  creation can take years and burn budget. Most products should win an existing
  category, not invent one. Treat a new category as a last resort, not a default move.
- The framework is qualitative. It gives you a defensible choice, not a number. It will
  not tell you market size or revenue. Pair it with sizing work elsewhere.
- It is commonly misapplied as a messaging or copywriting exercise. Positioning is the
  strategic choice of context. Messaging is downstream. Writing taglines before doing
  the five steps produces clever words for the wrong frame.
- B2B bias. The examples and the sales-cycle lens skew enterprise and B2B SaaS. For
  consumer products with impulse buys, the alternatives step still helps, but the
  deliberate-category logic matters less.

## Conflicts with other frameworks

- Blue Ocean / category creation thinking pushes you to invent uncontested space.
  Obviously Awesome pushes you to win an existing frame buyers already understand. Resolve
  by buyer maturity: if buyers have strong mental models and known alternatives, position
  inside an existing category. If buyers have no comparison and the value is genuinely
  novel, a new sub category may be justified, accept the cost.
- buy-vs-build-framework can conflict on sequence. That skill asks whether to build the
  capability at all. This skill assumes the capability exists and asks how to frame it.
  Resolve by running buy-vs-build first for the build decision, then this skill once the
  product is real. Do not use positioning to justify building something nobody needs.

## Example

A team ships a tool that watches cloud spend and flags waste. They default to the
category "cloud cost management" and stall: buyers compare them to incumbents with
broad budgeting suites and ask why they cost the same with fewer features.

Run the framework. Competitive alternatives (step 1): incumbent cost suites, a
homegrown spreadsheet, and a part-time FinOps analyst. Unique attributes (step 2):
real-time anomaly detection, automatic root-cause to the service that spiked, and a
one-click rollback. Value (step 3): waste caught in hours instead of at month-end
close, and the fix applied without a ticket. Who cares most (step 4): fast-scaling
engineering teams with no dedicated FinOps headcount, where a single runaway service
can blow the monthly budget.

Category choice (step 5): not "cloud cost management" (where buyers expect a full
budgeting suite and judge them on breadth), but "cloud anomaly detection" (where buyers
expect speed and precision, exactly the team's strengths). Trend (step 6): tie to the
shift toward autonomous remediation, which buyers already trust.

Result: the same product, reframed. Buyers stop comparing it to broad suites on feature
count and start comparing it to slow manual review on speed, where it wins. Score on the
checklist moved from 3 (no deliberate category, weak audience) to 6.
