# Crossing the Chasm

A go-to-market method for technology products that sold to visionaries but stalled
before the mainstream. The fix is focus: win one narrow segment so completely that
its references pull the next segment in.

## When to use

Use this when a product has early-adopter traction but mainstream growth has flatlined,
and the team is tempted to chase every interested buyer at once. Decision cue: if you
cannot name the single customer segment you would bet the company on this quarter, you
are in the chasm and need this skill.

## Decision tree

- If you have no paying customers yet and no product, then this is too early. Validate
  the problem first (see customer-discovery skills); the chasm is a scaling problem,
  not a search-for-fit problem.
- If sales come only from visionaries who tolerate a rough product, then you are at the
  edge of the chasm. Start the Core framework now, before momentum decays.
- If revenue is spread thin across many unrelated segments with no repeat references,
  then you are stuck in the chasm. Stop expanding. Pick one beachhead and concentrate.
- If one segment already buys repeatedly and refers peers, then you may have crossed.
  Shift to adjacent-segment expansion ("bowling pin" moves), not a new beachhead.
- If the product is a cheap, viral, self-serve consumer app, then this framework fits
  poorly. It was built for high-consideration B2B and infrastructure sales (see Honest
  limitations).

## Core framework

1. Accept the chasm. Early adopters (visionaries) buy a vision and tolerate risk. The
   early majority (pragmatists) buy a proven, complete solution and trust peer
   references. These are different buyers with different motivations. The gap between
   them is the chasm.
2. Target a single beachhead segment. Choose one narrow, nameable group with a shared,
   urgent problem your product solves better than anything else. Narrow is the point:
   a small market you dominate beats a large market where you are one of many.
3. Build the whole product. Pragmatists need the complete solution, not the core tech:
   integrations, support, documentation, services, partners. Fill every gap that blocks
   a pragmatist from getting full value out of the box.
4. Position around the compelling reason to buy. Anchor against the alternative the
   segment uses today. State who it is for, what category it competes in, and the one
   reason this segment must switch now.
5. Win references and dominate. Land a critical mass of reference customers inside the
   one segment so pragmatists hear "people like me already use this." Market-leader
   status inside a niche is the asset that lets you cross.
6. Expand to adjacent segments. Once you own the beachhead, use it as a reference base
   to knock over the next related segment, then the next. Each win makes the following
   easier.

## Scored checklist

Score each yes as the points shown. Total 10. Pass threshold: 7 or higher means the
beachhead is defensible enough to commit. Below 7, narrow further or pick a different
segment.

1. Can you name the beachhead segment in one phrase a stranger understands? (2 pts)
2. Does the segment share a single urgent, expensive problem you solve best? (2 pts)
3. Have you defined the whole product, including support, integrations, and partners,
   not just the core tech? (1 pt)
4. Is there a compelling reason for this segment to buy now, not later? (1 pt)
5. Can you reach this segment through a known channel (a list, an event, a community)? (1 pt)
6. Are there enough reference-able buyers in it to reach critical mass? (1 pt)
7. Is the segment small enough that you can become the visible leader within it? (1 pt)
8. Do wins here open a credible path to one adjacent segment next? (1 pt)

## Honest limitations

- Built for high-consideration B2B and infrastructure products of the 1990s and 2000s.
  Self-serve, product-led, and viral consumer adoption can skip the chasm entirely; the
  reference-driven model fits poorly there.
- Modern platforms and ecosystems can cross several segments at once. Strict "one
  segment only" focus can leave network-effect value on the table.
- Picking the wrong beachhead is the silent failure mode. The framework assumes you can
  judge segment urgency correctly; it does not give you that judgment.
- It says little about pricing, unit economics, or whether the business is viable at
  scale. A dominated tiny segment can still be a dead end.
- "Whole product" can be read as gold-plating. Build only what blocks the pragmatist,
  not everything imaginable.

## Conflicts with other frameworks

- Blue Ocean Strategy: Blue Ocean pushes you to create uncontested new market space and
  appeal broadly; Crossing the Chasm pushes you to focus narrowly inside an existing
  category. Resolve by stage: use Crossing the Chasm to win a first beachhead now, then
  reach for Blue Ocean moves once you have a reference base and room to redraw the
  category boundaries.
- Lean Startup / build-measure-learn: Lean favors broad, fast experimentation across
  many directions; this skill demands you stop experimenting and commit to one segment.
  Resolve by maturity: run Lean discovery before product-market fit, then switch to
  beachhead focus once early adopters are buying and you are scaling into the majority.

## Example

A workflow-automation startup sells to ten kinds of teams: marketing, finance, IT,
HR, legal, and more. Visionary buyers in each signed up, but renewals are flat and
no segment refers peers. Applying the framework, the team scores segments and picks
mid-market IT operations teams as the beachhead: a shared, urgent problem (manual
incident triage burns on-call hours), a reachable channel (an existing IT-ops Slack
community and two conferences), and enough buyers to reach critical mass. They build
the whole product for that one segment: a PagerDuty integration, an on-call runbook
template library, and same-day support. Positioning becomes "incident triage
automation for mid-market IT ops, the alternative to manual escalation." Within two
quarters, fifteen IT-ops teams are public reference customers, and pragmatist buyers
start arriving on word of mouth. Only then does the team open the adjacent segment,
DevOps platform teams, reusing the IT-ops references to shorten the next sales cycle.
