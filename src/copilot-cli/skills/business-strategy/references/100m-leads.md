# $100M Leads

Distilled from Alex Hormozi's "$100M Leads." Original framing in our own words. This is a reference skill: read it, decide, act. No scripts to run.

## When to use

Use this when you have something to sell but not enough people seeing or wanting it. The decision cue: you can name a product and a price, but your weekly count of qualified conversations is low, erratic, or unknown.

Do not use this to fix a broken offer (the product nobody wants once they see it) or to close deals already in the pipeline. Those are different problems.

## Decision tree

Route to the right move based on your situation.

- If you have an audience already (list, followers, past customers) and want fast volume, then run warm outreach and post free content to that audience first. Cheapest leads you will ever get.
- If you have money but no audience, then buy attention: paid ads or paid placements. Fastest to scale, costs cash, needs a tracked funnel.
- If you have time but no money and no audience, then run cold outreach (DMs, email, calls). Slow per lead, scales with effort, builds skill.
- If you are already getting leads from one channel and want more without more of your own labor, then activate Lead Getters: customers, employees, affiliates, and agencies who advertise for you.
- If leads come in but few convert, then stop adding channels. The bottleneck is the offer or the follow-up, not lead volume. Go back to offer work.
- If you do not know your numbers (leads per week, cost per lead, lead-to-sale rate), then instrument before you spend. You cannot improve a channel you do not measure.

## Core framework

The Core Four are the only four ways to get a stranger to engage. Everything else is a variation.

1. Pick a channel from the Core Four. Warm outreach (people who know you, one to one). Post free content (people who know you, one to many). Cold outreach (strangers, one to one). Paid ads (strangers, one to many). Start with the one your situation favors per the decision tree.
2. Make a Grand Slam Offer the lead magnet promises. Give away something genuinely valuable for free or cheap that solves a narrow problem and reveals the next, bigger problem only your paid product solves. The free thing must work on its own.
3. Do more. Run the chosen channel at higher volume than feels comfortable. Most channels fail from too little activity, not the wrong channel. Set a daily or weekly action quota and hit it.
4. Do better. Once volume is steady, improve the conversion of each step: the hook, the offer, the call to action, the follow-up. Test one variable at a time.
5. Add Lead Getters once one channel works. Recruit four types to advertise for you: customers (referrals), employees, affiliates, and agencies. Each multiplies reach without multiplying your own hours.
6. Measure, then scale the winner. Track leads, cost per lead, and lead-to-sale rate per channel. Pour budget and time into the channel with the best return. Keep the others warm.

## Scored checklist

Score your current lead engine. Each yes is worth points.

- [ ] You can name your single best Core Four channel and why it fits your situation (2 pts)
- [ ] You have a free or cheap lead magnet that solves a real problem on its own (2 pts)
- [ ] You hit a written weekly action quota on your primary channel (1 pt)
- [ ] You know your leads-per-week number from memory (1 pt)
- [ ] You know your cost per lead and lead-to-sale rate per channel (2 pts)
- [ ] At least one Lead Getter (customer, employee, affiliate, agency) sends you leads (2 pts)
- [ ] You ran one conversion test (hook, offer, or follow-up) in the last 30 days (1 pt)

Total: 11. Pass threshold: 7. Below 7 means your lead engine is improvised, not built. Fix the lowest-scoring rows first, starting with measurement and the lead magnet.

## Honest limitations

- Volume-first advice assumes a working offer. If the product is wrong, more leads burn more goodwill and more ad spend. The book leans hard on "do more" before "do better," which wastes money for founders whose conversion is broken at the root.
- The Lead Getter model (affiliates, agencies) fits high-ticket or high-margin products. For low-margin or low-price products, the economics of paying others to advertise often do not clear. Run the math before recruiting Lead Getters.
- The tactics are tuned for direct-response, info-product, and service businesses. Long sales cycles, enterprise B2B with committees, and regulated industries need adaptation; cold DMs do not move a six-month procurement cycle.
- Channel specifics age fast. Platform rules, ad costs, and outreach norms shift. Treat the named tactics as examples of the Core Four, not as a fixed playbook.

## Conflicts with other frameworks

- Versus an offer or pricing framework (the kind focused on willingness-to-pay or value design): if your scored offer is weak, that framework wins. Do not run high-volume lead gen against an offer nobody buys. Sequence: fix the offer, then open the channels.
- Versus a brand or positioning framework: those favor patience, narrow audience, and message before reach. This skill favors reach and volume first. Resolve by stage: pre-product-market-fit and cash-constrained, follow this skill for fast signal; post-fit and defending a category, let positioning lead and use this skill only to scale the proven channel.

## Example

A solo consultant sells a $4,000 leadership coaching package. Pipeline: two discovery calls a month, source unknown.

Decision tree: she has 1,400 LinkedIn connections (a warm audience) and no ad budget. Route is warm outreach plus free content to that audience.

Core framework applied:

1. Channel: warm outreach (one to one) plus weekly post (one to many).
2. Lead magnet: a free 20-minute "first 90 days as a new manager" teardown call. It helps on its own and surfaces the deeper coaching need.
3. Do more: quota of 10 personalized warm messages a day and 3 posts a week.
4. Do better: after two weeks, she swaps the message hook from "want coaching?" to "want a free teardown of your first 90 days?" Reply rate rises.
5. Lead Getter: she asks each past client for one introduction (customer referrals).
6. Measure: she tracks messages sent, replies, calls booked, and calls-to-sale.

Result after one month: 200 warm messages, 24 replies, 9 teardown calls, 2 closes. Discovery-call volume quadruples, and she now knows which step (replies to booked calls) leaks most, so next month she tests the booking flow rather than adding a new channel.
