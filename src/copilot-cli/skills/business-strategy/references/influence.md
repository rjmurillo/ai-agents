# Influence

## When to use

Reach for this when you have a specific ask (a yes, a signature, a referral, a hire, a purchase) and the other side is undecided or leaning away. Decision cue: if you can name the exact behavior you want and the person who must do it, run this. If you are still discovering what you want, stop and define the ask first.

## Decision tree

- If the other side owes you nothing and there is no relationship yet, then lead with Reciprocity: give first, with no strings named.
- If they have already said or done something small in your direction, then lead with Commitment: restate their prior position and ask for the next consistent step.
- If they are uncertain and similar people have already chosen, then lead with Social Proof: show peers like them who said yes.
- If you are a stranger to them, then lead with Liking: find a real shared point and surface it before the ask.
- If your competence is unproven to them, then lead with Authority: present credible, checkable evidence of expertise, not titles alone.
- If the offer is genuinely time- or supply-limited, then lead with Scarcity: state the real deadline and what is lost by waiting.
- If you share a group identity (team, alumni, region, mission), then lead with Unity: name the shared "we" before you ask.
- If you cannot honestly invoke any principle, then you do not have a persuasion problem. You have a value problem. Fix the offer.

## Core framework

The seven principles, applied as a sequence:

1. **Define the ask.** One sentence: who does what by when. Vague asks cannot be made persuasive.
2. **Pick the lead principle.** Use the decision tree. Match the principle to the relationship state, not to your preference.
3. **Earn the trigger honestly.** Each principle has a real-world basis: Reciprocity needs a genuine gift, Authority needs real expertise, Scarcity needs a true limit. Manufacturing the trigger is manipulation and it breaks on contact.
4. **Sequence, do not stack blindly.** Lead with one principle. Add a second only if it reinforces the first (Liking plus Unity), not if it competes for attention (Scarcity plus Reciprocity confuses the frame).
5. **Reduce friction on the yes.** Make the requested action small, concrete, and immediate. A persuaded person who cannot find the button stays a no.
6. **State the cost of no.** Name what the person loses by not acting, in their terms, without inflation.
7. **Leave the exit open.** "You're free to decide" raises compliance because it removes the threat of coercion. Never trap.

## Scored checklist

Score the message before you send it. Each yes is worth the points shown.

- [ ] The ask is one sentence: a named actor, a named action, a deadline. (2 pts)
- [ ] Exactly one lead principle is identified and matches the relationship state. (2 pts)
- [ ] The principle's trigger is real, not staged (real gift, real scarcity, real expertise). (3 pts)
- [ ] At most one supporting principle, and it reinforces rather than competes. (1 pt)
- [ ] The yes action is one concrete step the reader can take now. (1 pt)
- [ ] The cost of inaction is stated in the reader's terms, not inflated. (1 pt)
- [ ] The reader is explicitly free to say no. (1 pt)
- [ ] No false claim, hidden deadline, or fabricated social proof. (1 pt)

Total: 12. Pass threshold: 9. Below 9, the message will read as pressure or noise. A score that passes only by failing the "trigger is real" or "no false claim" items does not pass. Those two are gates, not points.

## Honest limitations

- The principles describe heuristics people use under low attention. A motivated, analytical reader who scrutinizes your claim is moved by substance, not triggers. Do not over-rely on this with experts evaluating you on merits.
- The research base skews to Western consumer studies from the 1980s to 2000s. Effect sizes vary by culture, channel, and decade. Treat each principle as a hypothesis to test, not a law.
- Stacking is the common misapplication. Beginners pile on all seven and dilute every one. One clean principle beats six muddy ones.
- Used to push a weak offer, these tactics raise short-term compliance and destroy long-term trust. The retraction (refund, churn, bad referral) costs more than the win.
- Social Proof and Scarcity are the most faked and the most detected. Fabricating either is the fastest way to be branded a manipulator.

## Conflicts with other frameworks

- **Negotiation** (`business-strategy/negotiation` style deal analysis): negotiation says slow down, quantify, never match urgency. Influence's Scarcity principle says use a real deadline to compress the decision. Resolve by situation: in an adversarial deal with a counterparty protecting their own value, negotiation wins; suppress your Scarcity play and let them set no false clock either. In a low-stakes one-shot ask to a willing party, Influence's Scarcity is fine.
- **Decision-rigor / pre-mortem framing**: rigor frameworks slow a decision to surface hidden risk; Influence speeds a decision to capture a yes. When the stakes are high and irreversible for the person you are persuading, defer to rigor. Do not use Scarcity or Commitment to rush someone past a decision they should make slowly. Persuasion ethics and decision quality point the same way here: help them decide well, then ask.

## Example

Ask: get a busy senior engineer to review your design doc by Friday.

Weak version: "Hey, can you review my doc when you get a chance? No rush." Score: ask has no deadline (0), no principle (0). Fails.

Strong version, leading with Reciprocity plus Liking:

"I used your caching write-up from last quarter to fix the latency bug on our service. Saved us a rough on-call week. I'm returning the favor in advance: I reviewed your team's API proposal and left three notes in the thread. Could you spend 20 minutes on my design doc by Friday? I ship the migration Monday, so Thursday feedback is the last point I can still act on it. Totally fine if you can't this week."

Checklist: named actor, action, deadline (2). One lead principle, Reciprocity, matched to no prior relationship (2). The gift is real, you actually reviewed their proposal (3). One supporting principle, Liking via the genuine shared work (1). Yes action is concrete, 20 minutes on a linked doc (1). Cost of no is stated in their frame, you lose the chance to act on it (1). Exit is open (1). No false claims (1). Total 12. Passes.
