# Traction: Bullseye Channel Selection

A reference for choosing how to acquire customers. Distilled from Traction by
Gabriel Weinberg and Justin Mares. The book's claim: most startups die from lack
of distribution, not lack of product. Founders default to the channel they know
instead of testing the channel that fits. Bullseye fixes that with cheap parallel
tests before you commit a budget.

## When to use

Reach for this when you have something to sell and need to decide where growth
comes from, or when your current channel has plateaued and you are guessing at
the next one. Decision cue: if you cannot name your single highest-leverage
acquisition channel and the cost per customer through it, you are not ready to
scale spend. Run Bullseye first.

## Decision tree

- If you have no paying users yet, then run the Inner Ring tests to find any
  channel that converts, not the cheapest one. Survival beats efficiency.
- If one channel already works but growth has stalled, then re-run Bullseye to
  find the next channel. Channels saturate; the one that got you here rarely gets
  you to the next stage.
- If you have a clear winning channel and a positive unit economic, then stop
  testing and optimize that channel. Bullseye is for discovery, not for steady
  state.
- If you cannot fund even small parallel tests, then narrow to the three channels
  your customers already inhabit and test those one at a time.
- If your product is a marketplace or has network effects, then weight viral and
  community channels higher; a single-sided paid channel can mislead you.

## Core framework

The Bullseye method runs in five steps.

1. Brainstorm every channel. List all 19 traction channels (viral marketing,
   PR, unconventional PR, SEM, social and display ads, offline ads, SEO, content
   marketing, email marketing, engineering as marketing, target market blogs,
   business development, sales, affiliate programs, existing platforms, trade
   shows, offline events, speaking engagements, community building). For each,
   write one plausible idea for how it could work for you. Force yourself to fill
   all 19; the channel you dismiss is often the one that wins.
2. Rank into three rings. Outer ring: long shots. Middle ring: possible. Inner
   ring: the two or three channels that look most promising right now.
3. Test the Inner Ring in parallel. Run cheap, fast, concurrent experiments on
   the inner three. Each test answers three questions: roughly what does a
   customer cost here, how many customers are reachable, and are these the right
   customers. Keep tests small enough to run in days, not quarters.
4. Pick one channel. Promote the test winner to the center of the bullseye.
   Focus is the point. One channel usually drives the bulk of growth at any
   stage; splitting effort across many starves the one that works.
5. Optimize until it taps out, then re-run. Pour effort into the chosen channel
   until its returns flatten, then return to step 2 with fresh data. Treat
   channel strategy as a loop, not a one-time choice.

Two rules sit under the method. Run the 50 percent rule: spend about half your
effort on product and half on traction from day one, because distribution is a
build, not an afterthought. And define your traction goal as a number (users,
revenue, or a critical-path metric) so a test result has a clear pass or fail.

## Scored checklist

Score before you commit a channel budget. Each yes is worth points.

- [ ] You brainstormed all 19 channels with one concrete idea each, not just the
  two you already favor. (2 pts)
- [ ] You named an explicit traction goal as a number with a deadline. (2 pts)
- [ ] You selected two or three Inner Ring channels with a written reason for
  each. (1 pt)
- [ ] You designed each Inner Ring test to be cheap and to finish in days. (1 pt)
- [ ] Each test estimates cost per customer, reachable volume, and customer fit.
  (2 pts)
- [ ] You will commit to one winning channel rather than splitting effort across
  several. (1 pt)
- [ ] You set a re-evaluation trigger (a metric or date) for when to re-run
  Bullseye. (1 pt)

Total: 10 points. Pass threshold: 7. Below 7, you are likely defaulting to a
familiar channel or scaling spend on an unvalidated one.

## Honest limitations

- The 19-channel list is from 2014. Newer surfaces (short-form video, influencer
  and creator partnerships, app-store optimization, community-led growth on
  Discord and Slack, marketplace ads) are folded awkwardly into the original
  buckets. Map your real channel to the nearest bucket; do not skip it because the
  book lacks a name for it.
- Bullseye optimizes for discovery, not for compounding. It tells you which
  channel works now, not which channel builds a durable moat. SEO and content
  compound slowly and will lose a short parallel test to paid ads even when they
  are the better long-run bet. Weight by time horizon, not just test speed.
- The method assumes channels are roughly independent. In practice content feeds
  SEO, PR feeds referrals, and a single test underrates a channel that only pays
  off in combination.
- "Pick one channel" is right early and wrong late. A scaled company runs a
  portfolio. Do not over-apply the focus rule once one channel is mature.
- Cost-per-customer from a tiny test is noisy. A channel can look dead at small
  scale and thrive at large scale, or the reverse. Treat one test as a signal,
  not a verdict.

## Conflicts with other frameworks

- Lean Startup (Build-Measure-Learn) can pull you toward endless product
  iteration while traction starves. Resolution: the 50 percent rule. If you have
  product-market signal but no distribution plan, Traction wins the next sprint;
  if the product still does not retain anyone, Lean Startup's validated-learning
  loop comes first.
- Crossing the Chasm says to dominate one narrow beachhead segment before you
  expand. That can conflict with Bullseye picking a broad channel like paid ads
  that reaches many segments thinly. Resolution: by stage. Pre-chasm, let segment
  focus constrain which channels you even test; a channel that cannot reach your
  beachhead is out regardless of its cost-per-customer.

## Example

A two-person B2B SaaS sells an invoicing tool to freelance designers. Their
traction goal: 100 paying customers in 90 days. They brainstorm all 19 channels
and rank three into the Inner Ring: target-market blogs (designers read a handful
of well-known blogs), SEM (clear high-intent search terms exist), and content
marketing (they can write).

They run three parallel two-week tests. Blogs: two sponsored posts, 600 dollars,
yields 9 trials and 2 paying customers, so about 300 dollars per customer with
strong fit. SEM: 500 dollars of Google ads, yields 40 clicks, 6 trials, 1 paying
customer, so 500 dollars per customer with mixed fit (many clicks were employees
at agencies, not freelancers). Content: one cornerstone post, near-zero cash but
40 hours, yields 3 trials and 0 paying customers inside the window.

Channel cost favors blogs, fit favors blogs, and SEM's audience skews wrong.
Content shows zero short-term return but is the only compounding option. They pick
target-market blogs as the center of the bullseye, scale to six blogs, and hit 70
of their 100 customers there. They keep one writer on content as a long-horizon
bet, flagged explicitly as not subject to the same short-test pass or fail. When
blog returns flatten near month four, they re-run Bullseye and promote SEO, which
content has now seeded.
