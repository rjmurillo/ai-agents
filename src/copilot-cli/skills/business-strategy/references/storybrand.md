# StoryBrand Messaging

Distilled from Donald Miller, Building a StoryBrand. Original wording, not the book text.

## When to use

Reach for this when your message is fuzzy and people do not understand what you sell or why they should care. Decision cue: if a stranger cannot repeat your value in one sentence after reading your homepage, you have a messaging problem, not a product problem.

## Decision tree

- If you have no clear message at all, run the full Core framework below and write a one-liner first.
- If you have a message but conversion is flat, audit which of the 7 parts is missing or weak. Usually the missing piece is a clear call to action or a named problem.
- If your copy talks mostly about you (your story, your awards, your tech), stop. The customer is the hero, not you. Rewrite from their desire.
- If the problem is pricing, distribution, or product fit, this is the wrong tool. Messaging cannot fix a product nobody wants.
- If you need to position against rivals or pick a market wedge, use a positioning framework first, then return here to phrase it.

## Core framework

The 7 parts, in order. Each maps to how a customer's brain processes a story.

1. A character. The customer is the hero. Open by naming what they want. One concrete desire, not a list.
2. Has a problem. Name three layers: the external problem (the tangible obstacle), the internal problem (how it makes them feel), and the philosophical problem (why it is just wrong that they should suffer it). Internal is the one most brands skip and the one that sells.
3. And meets a guide. You are the guide, not the hero. Show two things: empathy (you understand their pain) and authority (you have helped others solve it). Proof, testimonials, numbers.
4. Who gives them a plan. People do not buy until they see clear steps. Give a 3-step process or an agreement that removes risk. Name the steps.
5. And calls them to action. Make one direct call to action obvious and repeated (buy, book, start). Add a transitional call to action (download, free trial) for people not ready yet.
6. That helps them avoid failure. Name the stakes. What does the customer lose if they do nothing? Without stakes, the story is flat. Use sparingly so you warn, not nag.
7. And ends in success. Paint the after state. Show the specific, concrete win they get. Close the story the customer's brain is trying to finish.

Then compress all 7 into a one-liner: "We help [hero] who struggle with [problem] by [plan] so they can [success]."

## Scored checklist

Score each yes as the points shown. Total 10. Pass at 7 or higher.

- The customer, not the brand, is named as the hero in the first screen of copy. (2 pts)
- An internal problem (a feeling) is named, not just the external obstacle. (2 pts)
- The brand shows both empathy and authority as the guide. (1 pt)
- A plan with named steps (3 or fewer) is visible before the ask. (1 pt)
- One direct call to action is repeated and unmistakable. (2 pts)
- The cost of inaction (stakes) appears at least once. (1 pt)
- A concrete success state is described, not a vague promise. (1 pt)

If you score 6 or below, the weakest zero-point items are your rewrite list. Fix the highest-point gaps first.

## Honest limitations

- It is a wrapper, not a strategy. StoryBrand sharpens words. It does not find your market, set your price, or validate demand. A crisp message for a bad product just helps people reject you faster.
- The 7-part formula can feel templated. Run enough brands through it and they start to sound the same. Use it as scaffolding, then add a real voice.
- The fear and stakes step invites manipulation. Overplaying loss reads as scammy and erodes trust. Keep stakes honest and proportional.
- Weak in complex B2B sales with many buyers and long cycles. A single hero and single one-liner oversimplify a committee. Use it per persona, not once for all.
- It assumes the customer already knows their desire. In genuinely new categories, you must first teach the problem before the hero arc lands.

## Conflicts with other frameworks

- Jobs to Be Done (if that skill is in the pack): JTBD says start from the job the customer hires you for, which can de-center the emotional hero arc StoryBrand leads with. Resolve by situation. Use JTBD to discover the true desire and problem, then use StoryBrand to phrase the message around them. JTBD finds the content; StoryBrand finds the words.
- Positioning or category design frameworks: those tell you to differentiate sharply against rivals, while StoryBrand tells you to mirror the customer's existing language. They clash when standing out requires unfamiliar terms. Resolve by sequencing. Position first to pick the wedge, then use StoryBrand to translate that wedge into customer-facing copy.

## Example

A SaaS tool for solo bookkeepers, homepage rewrite.

- Hero: a solo bookkeeper who wants to close the month without working weekends.
- Problem: external is manual reconciliation across five bank feeds; internal is the dread of falling behind; philosophical is that skilled people should not lose Saturdays to copy-paste.
- Guide: empathy ("we built this after watching our own books eat our weekends") plus authority ("trusted by 4,200 bookkeepers, 4.8 stars").
- Plan: 1, connect your banks. 2, approve the auto-matches. 3, export a clean ledger.
- Direct call to action: "Start your first reconciliation free." Transitional: "Download the month-end checklist."
- Failure avoided: "Stop losing every Saturday to spreadsheets."
- Success: "Close the month by Friday at 5 and take your weekend back."

One-liner: "We help solo bookkeepers drowning in manual reconciliation auto-match their banks in three steps, so they close the month by Friday and keep their weekends."

Score: hero named (2), internal problem (2), guide empathy and authority (1), named plan (1), direct call to action repeated (2), stakes (1), concrete success (1) = 10/10. Pass.
