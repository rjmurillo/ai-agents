# Blue Ocean Strategy

A reference for deciding whether to fight for share in a known market (a red ocean) or create a new market with little competition (a blue ocean), and how to design the move so the new space is hard to copy.

## When to use

Use this when you are positioning a product or company and the obvious play is to beat existing rivals on the standard buying criteria, but you suspect the better play is to redefine what the market values. Decision cue: if your pitch is "like X but cheaper or better," stop and check whether a blue ocean move beats a head-on fight.

## Decision tree

Route by your situation:

- If the market is empty or you are the only credible player, you already have a blue ocean. Skip the framework. Focus on adoption and a moat, not category creation.
- If you are a small entrant against entrenched incumbents and cannot win on their terms, run the Core framework. A blue ocean move sidesteps their strengths.
- If you have product-market fit and want to grow share in a defined category, this is a red ocean problem. Use competitive positioning, not this skill. Out-execute on the criteria buyers already weigh.
- If buyers complain about a forced tradeoff that everyone in the category accepts (price versus quality, simple versus powerful), that tradeoff is the seam. Run the Core framework to break it.
- If you can name a large group of non-customers (people who refuse the whole category, not just your product), run the Core framework. Non-customers are where blue oceans hide.
- If the new space you imagine has no buyers willing to pay, it is not a blue ocean. It is a desert. Validate demand before you build.

## Core framework

1. Map the current state of play. List the factors the industry competes on (the standard buying criteria) and plot how each rival scores. This is the strategy canvas. If every rival has the same shape, the market is a red ocean.

2. Look outside current customers. Find the non-customers: people who use a substitute, refuse the category, or were never targeted. Ask what stops them. Their reasons reveal value the category ignores.

3. Apply the four actions to the factors:
   - Eliminate: which factors the industry takes for granted can you drop entirely?
   - Reduce: which factors can you cut well below the standard?
   - Raise: which factors should you push well above the standard?
   - Create: which factors that the industry never offered can you add?

4. Build a new value curve from those four actions. A real blue ocean move has a shape that diverges from rivals, a clear focus (it does not try to be strong on every factor), and a tagline a buyer can repeat.

5. Test for value innovation: the move must lift buyer value and cut your cost at the same time. If raising value forces your cost up in lockstep, you have a differentiation play in a red ocean, not a blue ocean.

6. Sequence the move: buyer utility first, then a price the mass of target buyers will pay, then a cost structure that hits profit at that price, then adoption hurdles. If any link fails, rework the idea before launch.

7. Plan the moat. A blue ocean attracts imitators. Lock in scale, network effects, brand, or cost advantage so the first mover keeps the space.

## Scored checklist

Score the candidate move. Each yes is worth the points shown.

1. Can you name the specific factor you will eliminate or reduce far below the industry norm? (2 points)
2. Can you name a factor you will create that no rival currently offers? (2 points)
3. Does the move both raise buyer value and lower your cost (value innovation, not just differentiation)? (3 points)
4. Can you name a large pool of non-customers the move converts? (2 points)
5. Does your value curve diverge clearly from every major rival's, with a focused shape? (2 points)
6. Is there a one-line tagline a target buyer would repeat unprompted? (1 point)
7. Have you validated that buyers will pay at a price your cost structure supports? (2 points)
8. Do you have a moat (scale, network, brand, cost) to hold the space after imitators arrive? (1 point)

Total: 15 points. Pass threshold: 10 or higher. Below 10, the move is likely a red ocean play dressed up as category creation, or a market with no paying demand. Rework before committing.

## Honest limitations

- Survivorship bias. The book's cases are chosen because they won. It does not count the firms that tried a blue ocean move, found no demand, and died. A clever value curve is not proof of a market.
- It undersells execution and defense. Creating the space is step one. Most value goes to whoever defends it. The framework is thin on moats, which is why the checklist forces a moat question.
- Static snapshot. The strategy canvas captures one moment. Fast-moving markets shift the competing factors underneath you while you plan.
- Easy to misapply as "just be different." Differentiation that raises cost is not a blue ocean. The value-innovation test (lift value and cut cost together) is the real bar, and it is the part people skip.
- Weak on timing and capital. A blue ocean move often needs patient capital and a window before incumbents react. The framework does not tell you whether you can afford the wait.

## Conflicts with other frameworks

- Versus a competitive-positioning or Five Forces skill: that lens tells you to study rivals and win on the established criteria; this lens tells you to ignore rivals and change the criteria. Resolve by market maturity. In a young, fragmented market with unserved non-customers, prefer blue ocean. In a mature market where buyers agree on what matters and switching is costly, prefer positioning and out-execute on the known criteria.
- Versus a lean-startup or MVP-validation skill: lean says ship a thin slice and let real buyers steer you; blue ocean says design a divergent value curve up front. Resolve by uncertainty. When demand for the new value is unknown, lean validation comes first; use it to test checklist items 4 and 7 before you commit capital to the full curve. Blue ocean shapes the hypothesis; lean tests it.

## Example

A budget airline enters a market dominated by full-service carriers competing on lounges, meals, seat class, hub connections, and frequent-flyer tiers.

Strategy canvas: every full-service rival has the same shape, strong on every factor, priced high.

Four actions:
- Eliminate: lounges, meal service, seat-class tiers, assigned hubs.
- Reduce: ticket price, route complexity, in-flight frills.
- Raise: departure frequency on a few high-demand point-to-point routes, friendly fast boarding.
- Create: a single simple fare, no-frills self-serve booking, speed of turnaround.

Value innovation check: dropping meals, lounges, and tiers cuts cost sharply; raising frequency and simplicity lifts value for price-sensitive travelers. Value up, cost down. Pass.

Non-customers: people who drive or take the bus because flying feels expensive and stuffy. The simple cheap fare converts them.

Checklist score: eliminate factor named (2), created factor of a single flat fare and fast turnaround (2), value innovation holds (3), large non-customer pool of drivers and bus riders (2), divergent focused curve (2), tagline "cheap, simple, on time" (1), demand validated by route load tests (2), moat is cost leadership and scale on dense routes (1). Total 15. Above threshold. Commit, and defend with cost and scale before imitators copy the curve.
